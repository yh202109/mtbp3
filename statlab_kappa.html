StatLab/Cohen’s Kappa for inter-rater reliability

Background

Cohen’s kappa is a statistic used for describing, summarizing, estimating and testing inter-ratter consistency.

The observed raw percentage of agreement is defined as $$p_O = \sum_{j=1}^J N_{jj} / N_{\bullet\bullet}$$ where $J \geq 2$ is the size of value set.

Assume that $$(N_{1\bullet}, \ldots N_{J\bullet}) \sim multi(N_{\bullet \bullet}, (p_{r=1,1}, \ldots, p_{r=1,J})),$$ and $$(N_{\bullet 1}, \ldots N_{\bullet J}) \sim multi(N_{\bullet \bullet}, (p_{r=2,1}, \ldots, p_{r=2,J})),$$ with $\sum N_{j \bullet} = \sum N_{\bullet j} = N_{\bullet \bullet}$ and $\sum p_{r=1,j} = \sum p_{r=2, j} = 1$.

Under independence assumption, the expected number of agreement is estimated by $$ \sum_{j=1}^J\hat{E}{j} = \frac{1}{N{\bullet \bullet}}\sum_{j=1}^J N_{\bullet j} N_{j\bullet} \equiv N_{\bullet \bullet}p_E $$

The $\kappa$ statistic is calculated as $$\kappa = \frac{p_O - p_E}{1-p_E}$$ The SE of $\kappa$ is calculated as $$\sqrt{\frac{p_O(1-p_O)}{N_{\bullet \bullet}(1-p_E)^2}}$$

Interpretation in Literature

Cohen (1960)[1] suggested the Kappa result be interpreted as follows:

$0.6 < \kappa \leq 0.8$

Why?

These examples are created to emphasize the fact that Cohen’s kappa is a type of correlation measurement. In really, collected numbers won’t be as perfectly “uncorrelated” as shown in these examples above.
